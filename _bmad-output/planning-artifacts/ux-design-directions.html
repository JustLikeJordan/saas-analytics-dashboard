Step 9 — Design Directions

SaaS Analytics Dashboard

Six design directions exploring different visual treatments for the AI-first analytics experience. Each uses the same content, layout structure, and component hierarchy — the variation is in personality and visual tone.

Tone

Professional, familiar, corporate-friendly. What David expects a dashboard to look like.

Strengths

Zero learning curve. Immediately recognizable. Accessible. Safe choice for portfolio.

Weaknesses

Indistinguishable from 90% of SaaS dashboards. Doesn't communicate "AI-first." Could feel generic to hiring managers.

Best For

If blending in with enterprise expectations matters more than standing out.

Warm Advisory

Tone

Warm, human, advisory. Like a friend who's great with numbers explaining your books.

Strengths

Strongly differentiates from corporate blue dashboards. The left-border accent card communicates "this is a message from someone." Aligns with the product's advisory metaphor.

Weaknesses

Warm/orange palette is unusual for financial tools — could feel less "serious." Might trigger "this isn't a real business tool" for some evaluators.

Best For

If the "advisor over coffee" tone is the dominant design message and standing out from blue-SaaS matters most.

Direction 4

Minimal Editorial

Newsletter-inspired, typography-forward design. Almost no color — meaning comes from the text. Borders replace shadows. Underlines replace highlights. The text IS the design.

Tone

Editorial, intellectual, confident. "The Economist of dashboards." Assumes the reader values substance over decoration.

Strengths

Strongest alignment with "AI summary IS the product." Typography-forward means the text dominates. Unique in the dashboard space — stands out in portfolio review.

Weaknesses

Too minimal for users who expect a "dashboard" to look like a dashboard. Chart bars in black lack semantic color meaning. May feel cold or austere to Marcus.

Best For

If the reading experience is paramount and you want the boldest differentiation from typical SaaS dashboards.

Strong visual personality. Dark sidebar creates clear navigation hierarchy. Gradient bars add energy to charts. Feels polished.

Weaknesses

Gradients can feel dated quickly. Dark sidebar on light content creates high contrast that may fatigue. More "SaaS landing page" than "daily-use tool."

Best For

If the portfolio needs to feel like a funded startup product with strong visual branding.

Direction 6 — Recommended

Trust Blue

Aligns with the architecture's oklch token decisions. Trust-building blue primary, warm near-white backgrounds, balanced information density. Professional enough for hiring managers, approachable enough for Marcus.

Tone

Trustworthy, balanced, warm-professional. Like a well-designed financial app that happens to be smart.

Strengths

Directly maps to architecture oklch tokens. Blue primary builds trust (financial tool convention). Warm near-white background avoids clinical sterility. Balanced density works for both Marcus (quick glance) and David (weekly review). Portfolio-ready.

Weaknesses

Less visually striking than Direction 3 (Dark) or 4 (Editorial). "Trust blue" is common in fintech — differentiation comes from the AI content, not the chrome.

Best For

When the design should get out of the way and let the AI content shine. The "just right" option that balances all three audiences (Marcus, David, hiring manager).
